What the diversification change does

Plain-English explainer · prepared for the July 22, 2026 after-hours push

This change teaches the trading bot two things it did not know before: what a sector is, and what market weather is. It also adds five new stocks to the watchlist. Each piece has its own on/off switch and only turns on after a historical test approves it.

The problem, in one picture

One of the bot's strategies ("xsectmom") holds the 3 strongest stocks out of 63 and rotates as rankings change. The watchlist was carefully built with 14 different sectors — but the strategy picks purely by recent performance, and during an AI rally the three best performers are always chips. Change 1 — the sector cap

When the strategy picks its top 3, it now walks down the ranking and takes at most one stock per sector. The strongest chip still gets in; the second- and third-strongest chips are skipped in favor of the strongest stocks from other sectors. Every skipped name is written to the log, so you can always see what diversification cost that day.

The honest trade-off: during a rally in one sector, the capped version will earn less than the uncapped one — on purpose. It gives up some upside in the hottest sector to avoid being wiped out with it. Whether that trade is worth it is exactly what the historical test measures.

One thing to expect on day one: if the cap turns on while the bot still holds MU, AMD, and ARM, the next morning's 10:00 rotation will sell two of them and buy the strongest non-chip names. That is the change working as designed, not a malfunction.

Change 2 — the regime filter (noticing the weather)

Every strategy in this bot buys stocks and profits when they rise. None of them had any idea whether the overall market was healthy. The regime filter adds one shared reflex: while the S&P 500 is trading below its 200-day average — a common "bad weather" line — the bot stops opening new positions. Anywhere, in every strategy.

  • It only blocks new buys. Selling, stop-losses, and protective exits are never blocked — reducing risk is always allowed.
  • If it can't get the data, it stands aside. A broken safety check must never freeze the bot, so on any data problem the filter allows trading and complains loudly in the log instead.
  • You'll know when it flips. Every risk-on/risk-off change is logged, saved to the permanent audit file, and sent to your phone.

Change 3 — five new stocks

The watchlist already covered most sectors well. These five fill the genuinely thin spots — each one behaves differently from what's already there:

What decides whether each piece turns on

A replay tool re-runs the last two years of history and shows, in one table, what the cap, the filter, and the new names would have done — returns, worst drawdown, and how often the old approach held three copies of the same sector. Each piece is approved or rejected on its own numbers. Nothing turns on because it sounds like a good idea.

What does not change

  • How orders are placed, tracked, and reconciled — untouched.
  • The swing strategy stays benched; its shadow experiment continues.
  • Existing stop-losses and exits work exactly as before.
  • Crypto remains parked; nothing here touches it.

One sentence to remember: the watchlist was always diversified — this change makes the bot able to see it.
